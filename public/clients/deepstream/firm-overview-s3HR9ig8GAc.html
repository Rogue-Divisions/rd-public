02.
Our understanding

What we heard from DeepStream.

Here is our understanding of the company's position, based on preliminary conversations, to confirm or correct in a first working session. Two priorities came through clearly: capital, and retained control. A strategic partner would be valuable, but ranks behind the raise.

Capital
Growth funding of around £3–5M to press the advantage of a strong commercial quarter and rapidly rising ACV.
Control
Retaining ownership and operating control. An outright sale, or a structure that hands over control on day one, is not what the company wants now.
A strategic partner, second to the raise
Valuable, but secondary to the capital and control above. Ideally a strategic counterparty with a cross-sell ecosystem, who could take a majority position later and acquire the balance on an earn-out, rather than an all-or-nothing acquisition now.
The asset underneath it
A modern platform on a clean semantic layer with little technical debt, which is what has let DeepStream move up-market on AI while competitors on older, acquired codebases cannot.
03.
The tension

The core tension.

The offers a company receives at this stage tend to force a choice the board should not have to make: the capital that is easiest to close often carries the most control, and the buyers who move fastest tend to want all of it.

DeepStream's two priorities, capital and retained control, are not contradictory. They are served by running a process designed to hold both, not by taking the first complete offer on the table.

04.
The structure

The proposed structure: a raise first, with an optional second track.

The core of what we would run is the raise: capital now, on terms set by a competitive process, with control retained. The same engagement can also open a second, optional track, a managed search for the right long-term partner, pursued only if the board wants it. The raise comes first; the strategic partner, while valuable, sits a step behind it.

Track A · the priority
Near-term minority round
£3–5M · control retained
A focused, time-boxed minority round at the company's current momentum, so the board keeps control. The purpose is immediate: capital to amplify sales and marketing while the quarter is strong, on terms set by a competitive process rather than a single counterparty.
Track B · optional
Managed search for the strategic partner
Pursued only if the board wants it
If the board chooses to pursue it, a deliberate search for the strategic counterparty who fits the longer-term picture: a partner with a cross-sell ecosystem who could take a majority position at the right moment, with the balance on an earn-out. The eventual acquirer found through a real process, not because they happened to call first.

Both run from one set of materials and one narrative, and they inform each other: interest on Track B strengthens the terms on Track A, and capital from Track A buys time to run Track B without pressure.

05.
The fit

Why this fits DeepStream specifically.

An optional acquisition track only works when time is on the company's side, and for DeepStream it is, for a specific reason.

The most durable value in a software business like this is not the current revenue multiple. It is the compounding asset underneath: a clean, modern platform that turns customer activity into structured data and gets more defensible with each turn. DeepStream's semantic layer and low technical debt are exactly that, still priced on backward-looking multiples.

Used well, that compounding is something DeepStream can capture rather than sell away at today's number.

That trajectory makes the raise financeable now, and any later strategic deal more valuable: every quarter the flywheel compounds improves both the price and the company's position.

06.
Valuation

The headline is the floor, not the ceiling.

Headline SaaS multiples have compressed from the 10–15x revenue of three years ago to roughly 4–5x today.

10–15x
headline revenue multiple, three years ago.
4–5x
headline revenue multiple today — the start of the conversation.

Where a business lands within and above that range is decided by two things: the resilience of its distribution, and the strength of its data flywheel. DeepStream has a credible case on both.

As part of the engagement we produce a formal valuation mapping with a defensible range, and use a competitive process, not a single negotiation, to move the company up it. A board with banking experience knows the difference between a price taken and a price made.

Our process runs in five stages. Buildout is typically four to six weeks. A full process runs in the three-to-six-month range, and a minority-only round can move faster.

Every engagement runs against a live environment the board can see: pipeline, conversations, meetings, and milestones in real time, rather than a monthly report. The accountability is in the open.

08.
Commercials

Built to align with outcomes.

Our compensation is built to align with outcomes rather than inputs, and to flex to the shape of the deal. A straightforward minority raise carries a simple, conventional structure; a raise that later extends into a staged acquisition calls for terms built for that shape. We would set the specifics with you at scoping, once the structure is clear.

09.
Why a process

Choosing, rather than being chosen.

The risk in negotiating against a single counterparty is structural: that counterparty sets the frame, the price, and the pace. Our job is to remove that asymmetry.

We put several aligned, right-fit investors and acquirers against the same opportunity at once, so the company is choosing rather than being chosen, and the terms reflect competition rather than convenience.

That is the difference between DeepStream being sold and DeepStream being bought.

For a business with an asset that is still mispriced, it pays for the process several times over.
