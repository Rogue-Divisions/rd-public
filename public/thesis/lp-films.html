01.
The problem

The transmission problem.

Fund commitments are committee decisions. The principal in the room is rarely the only person who has to be persuaded. The decision must be defensible inside an investment committee, a board, a multi-generational family, or an external consultant review. The internal champion at the LP is not closing the allocation in the meeting. They are leaving with a story they will be expected to repeat, often imperfectly, weeks later, to people who never sat across from the GP.

Narrative degrades with every retelling. Rogue refers to this as the transmission problem. The deck is static and does not capture conviction. The data room is comprehensive and does not capture story. The first meeting captures both, briefly, in the body of one allocator. By the time that allocator is briefing their IC the following week, the thesis has been simplified, the differentiators smoothed, and the language has drifted toward whichever frame the allocator was already comfortable with.

The narrative the IC eventually evaluates is rarely the narrative the GP delivered.

Closing that gap is the job the kit was built to do.

02.
The artefact

Why film, and why a kit.

The standard raise stack performs every function except one: carrying the GP's voice, register, and conviction into the room where the GP is not. Film performs that function in a form no other artefact can match. It is also the asset most likely to be forwarded intact to an IC or co-investor, because the format itself resists summary distortion.

A clipped quote in an email can be paraphrased away. A two-minute film of the GP articulating the edge cannot. A single film is necessary but not sufficient. A kit is a film and the assets that compound around it.

05.
In market

What changes when the kit is in market.

Private capital attribution is multi-touch and multi-cycle. A single LP commitment frequently has nine to eighteen months of accumulated contact across owned content, conference encounters, warm introductions, and direct meetings before commitment lands. Attempting to attribute a single commitment to a single touchpoint is misleading. The kit is one input among many, and the manager's IC should hold that frame.

What the kit demonstrably changes is what travels with the LP after the first meeting.

01
The version of the thesis the LP carries into their internal review.
A film and a segmented cut sent in follow-up replaces the champion's reconstructed paraphrase with the manager's own articulation. The simplest test of whether the kit is working is whether the champion is forwarding the assets onward, requesting additional cuts for colleagues, or surfacing the thesis in their own language in subsequent conversations.

06.
Boundaries

The kit lands hardest where it is the missing piece.

The kit is the canonical owned-content artefact for a raise. It is not a comms strategy by itself.

Where it works
A manager with sound infrastructure
A manager with strong relationship infrastructure and a sound deck will see a step-change in transmission quality from the kit alone. The kit lands hardest where it is the missing piece in an otherwise functional raise.
Where it does less
A manager without supporting pieces
A manager with thin existing IR infrastructure, no public footprint, and no clear LP target list will see less of that change without supporting work. The kit is not where a raise starts from zero.

What the kit is not.

Not capital introduction
The kit equips placement agents, capital introduction, and warm-introduction channels. It does not replace them.
Not relationship building
Rogue does not introduce LPs, broker relationships, or make first contact on the manager's behalf.
Not compliance review
All assets are reviewed by the manager's fund counsel before distribution. The kit is built to clear counsel review efficiently.
Not regulatory advice
Rogue does not perform compliance review and does not opine on regulatory questions.
