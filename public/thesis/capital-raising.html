01.
The problem

The state of capital raising.

Raising capital is harder than it should be, and the standard playbook is not aging well. A founder with a strong business often spends six to nine months running an outreach motion that pre-supposes warm intros, generic decks, and the hope that the right investor will surface. Most do not. The ones who do tend to be a function of network, not narrative.

Two patterns repeat in conversations with founders who come to us after running their own process.

The volume problem.

Founders are sending the same pitch to investor lists that were not built around their deal. The reply rate is low because the targeting is generic. The signal is buried under noise. By the time a founder has burned through a hundred warm intros and a thousand cold emails, the remaining capacity is exhausted and morale is lower than the cap table can sustain.

The narrative problem.

The deck reads like the founder is in the room presenting it, but the founder is not in the room. Investors get five minutes with the asset. If the document does not communicate the thesis, the unit economics, the moat, and the path to the next round in the first thirty seconds, the deal is filed. Most decks are written for the founder, not the investor. Most teasers are too long, too generic, or too vague to drive a meeting.

These two problems compound. A poorly targeted list with a poorly framed asset produces poor outcomes regardless of how strong the underlying business is. The fix is not more activity. The fix is alignment between message and audience.

The investor list that worked five years ago will not close your round today.

The third pattern, larger than the first two.

Private capital is concentrating at the top and fragmenting at the bottom. The largest funds are raising at unprecedented scale and deploying into mega-rounds, particularly in AI infrastructure, that have nothing to do with most founders' lives. Below that, the actual deployment frontier for early-stage and lower-middle-market equity has fragmented across emerging managers, family offices, sovereigns, strategic capital, and corporate venture arms.

LPs are demanding distributions, not paper marks. Family offices are increasingly going direct. The implication for founders is uncomfortable but clear: getting in front of the capital that is actually deploying, rather than the capital that makes headlines, is harder than it has ever been, and the firms that can do it precisely are at a structural advantage.

That alignment, between message and audience, against the actual deployment frontier as it exists today, is what we call Investor-Message Fit.

02.
Our position

Becoming the familiar argument.

Capital does not flow to the best argument. It flows to the most familiar one, brought to the investor by a chain of trust they already recognise. Investor-Message Fit is the discipline of becoming the familiar argument for the investors who can actually back the deal.

Familiar means legible to the room that decides. Investors do not write cheques alone. The cheque is written after a partnership conversation, an investment committee defense, a portfolio review, an LP letter. The deal has to survive all of those. The numbers have to tie out. The narrative has to map to a thesis the partnership has already aligned around. The shape has to read as a deal this fund makes, not a deal this fund is being asked to consider for the first time.

Legibility to capital is not a presentation skill. It is the discipline of producing a deal an investor can defend, repeat, and underwrite without bending.

Legible does not mean identical. The strongest deals are legible in shape and unfamiliar in insight: an architecture the partnership pattern-matches, with a wedge inside it the partnership has not yet had to consider. The first makes the bet underwritable. The second makes it worth taking. Most fundraising goes wrong by getting one and not the other.

Mandate fit is the entry condition.

The investor's thesis, stage, sector, and cheque size have to align with the round, or no amount of positioning closes it. Mandate fit is the entry condition, not the win. Most fundraising effort never gets past it: the wrong investors look at the deal, decline politely, and the founder loses six months of runway working a list that was never built around the deal.

Past mandate fit, two questions.

Question one
How do we show this is a 100x opportunity?
The magnitude case. Building the evidence and the narrative so the right investors can see how this becomes the deal that returns the fund, not just an interesting business.
Question two
How do we de-risk it?
The process case. Running parallel right-fit investors against the same round so that interest compounds visibly, the round moves at a known pace, and the cost of inaction becomes sharper than the cost of action.

Most fundraising effort answers one of these questions, not both. We answer both at the same time.

The engine

01. Narrative.

Your investment thesis distilled from traction, vision, and team into materials an investor can scan in five minutes and underwrite in fifty. That means a sharper deck, a teaser that drives meetings rather than describes the business, a data room organised the way diligence actually flows, and a teaser-deck-meeting funnel where each step earns the next.

On nuanced deals, narrative does heavier lifting. Some of the strongest businesses we work with do not sit cleanly inside one investor bucket. The job of the narrative is to give an investment committee something they can take to their LPs and defend, even when the deal does not match their previous twenty deployments. That is craft.

02. Investor segment.

Not a list. A mandate-aware target set built from stage, sector, geography, cheque size, deal type, portfolio fit, and thesis alignment. Our database integrations, network mapping, and live deal-flow signals let us identify the investors actively deploying into deals like yours, not the investors who deployed into something adjacent in 2019.

Outreach is then prioritised in waves. The strongest-fit investors are reserved for the moment the materials and process are tight, not burned in the first wave when the calibration is still being learned.

03. Channel.

Different investor types form conviction in different places. Family offices and principal investors get there through events, personal references, and reputation that travels inside their peer set. Institutional VCs get there through warm introductions, founder narrative, and thesis-aligned content that reaches them where they already pay attention. Strategic and corporate venture investors get there through industry presence and partnership conversations. Emerging managers and thesis-led specialists get there through direct, well-framed approach from a founder who has clearly done the work.

Channel work is choosing where each segment of your investor universe will encounter the deal, and meeting them there. A campaign that produces fifteen of the right meetings beats one that produces a hundred wrong ones.

04. Presentation.

The materials and the founder both need to be ready for the meeting that the campaign generates. We refine the pitch deck and supporting collateral against real investor feedback, coach delivery, build out objection-handling for the questions we are seeing on calls, and prepare the data room so that diligence is fast rather than fraught.

We sit on calls when useful. We do not sit on calls when not. Either way the materials, the talk track, and the follow-up sequence are calibrated to the investor type in front of you.

The loop. Optimisation.

Every investor meeting produces signal. Which questions came up, which objections recurred, which segments are converting at a higher rate, where the narrative is strong, and where it needs another pass. We capture that signal, feed it back into the materials, the targeting, and the messaging, and run the next wave better than the last.

This is the part most advisory firms do not do, and it is the part that matters most. A first-wave reply rate is data. A second-wave reply rate that is double the first is leverage.

04.
The mechanism

Why it works.

The mechanism is plain when you separate the two halves of the investor decision. Belief in the upside has to be built. Urgency has to be compressed. Structure has to set up the round after this one.

Layer 01
Upside
Magnitude case
Belief in the upside is built by evidence framed inside a narrative the investor can repeat. We surface the strongest data your business has, unit economics, retention, market validation, proof of scale where applicable, and structure it inside a story that maps to how the right investors at your stage actually pattern-match deals. Materials that pass the first scan and survive the second meeting.
Layer 02
Urgency
Process case
An investor weighing your deal in isolation has all the time in the world. An investor weighing your deal alongside aligned interest from other right-fit investors does not. Parallel waves, real interest from real counterparties surfaced visibly, a managed pace through diligence. Make the cost of inaction sharper than the cost of action.
Layer 03
Structure
Second-order effect
Finding the right lead, not just any cheque, sets the rest of the round up to close. The right lead validates the deal for follow-on capital. The right cap table is structurally built to support the next round before you need to raise it. We work with founders on cap table construction, not just on capital identification.

Combined, those three layers shorten the time to a strong term sheet and improve the terms of the round when it arrives.

05.
The system

The system behind it.

The capital markets adopted email twenty years late and have been adopting modern operating practice ever since. The advisory firms that stayed dominant kept their relationships and their processes. The advisory firms that grew to fit the current environment built systems.

Rogue Divisions is built on integrated systems that compress the work an analyst used to do over two weeks into an afternoon, and the tasks that used to take an afternoon into something ambient. Investor intelligence, mandate matching, deal operations, and post-meeting analysis run as one stack rather than as a sequence of disconnected tools.

What that frees up is judgement: where to position the deal, which investors to reach, how to reframe the narrative when the first wave teaches us something the second wave should reflect.

The system is what makes our judgement go further than a traditional advisory team can without it. The output is what we are accountable for: meetings with the right investors, faster, on terms that compound to a better round.

We make that work visible. Every engagement runs against a live client environment that shows the round in real time: investor pipeline, conversation status, meetings booked, milestones, next steps. Founders see the work as it happens, not in a monthly update. The accountability is in the open.

The lessons are not from finance. They are from the operators we worked with before this was a capital markets firm: the best venture-backed startups, the best consumer brands, the best growth marketing teams. Those operators run tight feedback loops, instrument everything, segment relentlessly, and iterate against signal. We brought that discipline into a market that still defaults to relationship, list, and deck. That gap is the firm's edge, and it does not close on its own.

How we work

Our process.

The five-component framework above is what we are working towards. The process below is how we get there in practice. Buildout typically takes four to six weeks from kickoff to first wave; outreach and refinement run in parallel from that point through close.

01. Discovery and strategy.

  • Deep working session on the business model, market position, competitive landscape, growth thesis, and existing capital position.
  • Identification of investor objections we expect to see and the reinforcement work needed to pre-empt them.
  • Capital-raising strategy: which investors, which channels, which materials, which sequence, which milestones.

02. Materials and data.

  • Audit of existing pitch deck, data room, financial model, and any supporting collateral.
  • Refinement or rebuild of pitch materials, teaser document, executive summary, and data room structure.
  • Where applicable, targeted data acquisition campaigns to gather the metrics investors will ask for: customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, retention curves, market validation.
  • A unified teaser document that anchors all outbound messaging.

03. Investor research.

  • Mandate-driven targeting across our investor network and database integrations: stage, sector, geography, cheque size, deal type, portfolio fit, recent deployment activity.
  • A curated, prioritised investor list segmented into tiers, with the strongest-fit investors reserved for the moment process and materials are most calibrated.

04. Outreach and appointment setting.

  • Multi-wave outreach across email, LinkedIn, warm introductions, and where appropriate, paid distribution.
  • Personalisation at scale through our outreach infrastructure: each investor receives a message calibrated to their mandate, recent investments, and known thesis.
  • Inbox management on the founder's behalf. Investor replies are triaged, qualified, and warmed before introduction. Founder time is reserved for genuine conversations, not triage. Declines are captured and used to build relationships for future rounds.
  • Meetings booked directly to the founder's calendar.
  • Handling of compliance and regulatory considerations through our broker-dealer chaperone partnerships where the deal type requires it.

05. Iteration and close support.

  • Coaching on pitch delivery and objection-handling between meetings.
  • Recording and analysis of investor calls to identify recurring themes.
  • Continuous refinement of materials, targeting, and messaging based on signal from each wave. Refinement runs on three triggers: performance thresholds when a campaign underperforms, milestone gates as each tier completes, and a fixed time cadence regardless of either.
  • Support through term sheet, diligence, and close. We are partners through the round, not transactional through the first meeting.

Results you can expect.

Faster time to a strong term sheet.
By matching the deal to the right investor segment from the start, the conversion from first meeting to credible interest is materially compressed.
Better terms.
Reduced perceived risk and a competitive process between aligned investors translate to favourable valuations and structures.
More leverage.
Multiple aligned investors at the same table create the optionality to choose your lead and structure your cap table for the rounds that come after this one.

By the end of buildout your materials, data room, and investor narrative are in better shape than they were when you started, regardless of how the round closes. That work compounds.
08.
Right fit

Is Rogue Divisions right for you?

The honest answer is that we are a strong fit in some situations and a wrong fit in others.

Signals we are the right partner.

  1. 01
    You are raising equity at Seed, Series A, or Series B, with an established business or a credible thesis backed by data, and you have a clear use of proceeds.
  2. 02
    You have run a process and the targeting feels off. You are getting meetings with investors who do not understand the deal, or you are not getting meetings at all because the list was not built around your specifics.
  3. 03
    Your deal needs positioning work. The business is strong but does not sit cleanly inside one investor bucket, and you need someone who can translate it into language each segment of investors can underwrite.
  4. 04
    You value process and infrastructure. You want to know what is happening, when, and what the data is showing, rather than waiting for a monthly update from an advisor on a static retainer.
  5. 05
    You are looking for a partner, not a list. You want someone who will work the deal end to end and stays with you through the close, not someone who hands over an investor spreadsheet and waits for an introduction request.

Signals we are the wrong partner.

  1. ×
    You are pre-product and pre-thesis with no data and no clear use of proceeds. We are not the right firm to help you find the business.
  2. ×
    You are looking exclusively for cheap angel cheques inside your existing network. The marginal value of our process is lower in that situation.
  3. ×
    You are not prepared to commit time to building the materials and the targeting strategy with us in the first four to six weeks. The work compounds; the buildout matters.
09.
Commercial

Three legs of a stool.

Our pricing is built on three components. We treat them as three legs of a stool: increasing one allows the others to come down. We are flexible on the mix, deliberately, because every deal is structured differently. The alignment that works for a $5M Seed is not the alignment that works for a $50M Series B.

Leg 01
Setup fee
$10–25k
USD or GBP · one-time, upfront
Covers compliance and regulatory work, due diligence on our side, materials development, investor list curation, outreach infrastructure, analyst time, and the buildout phase that gets the campaign ready to go live. Not a retainer. Hard cost incurred on day one and recovered on day one.
Leg 02
Success fee
2–4%
Equity · 1–2% on debt
A percentage of capital raised through our introductions, paid on close. We do not take exclusive mandates; the success fee is paid on capital that comes through our process. Where appropriate, success fees can be split with co-advisors in our network when capital is raised through their relationships.
Leg 03
Warrants
0.5–1.5%
Most recent round price
Where useful, a portion of compensation is structured as warrants on the company at the most recent round price. Prorated to capital raised or time-vested over twelve to twenty-four months depending on the engagement. Warrants align our incentives with the long-term success of the business and reduce the cash burden.

How the three legs interact.

Minimise upfront cash
Shift weight onto success fee or warrants. Useful where buildout cash is tight but the deal is high-conviction.
Minimise dilution
Shift weight onto upfront and success. Useful where the cap table is sensitive or the next round depends on a clean ownership picture.
Deepest long-term alignment
Shift weight onto warrants and keep setup and success leaner. We hold equity alongside you and our incentive stays tied to the company's outcome long after the round closes.
Multi-round partnership
Six-month initial terms with renewal terms agreed if the round runs longer. Framed with a view to multiple rounds, because the strongest outcomes are partnership-shaped, not transactional.
